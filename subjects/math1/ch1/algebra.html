Introduction to Algebra

Algebra is a branch of mathematics dealing with symbols and the rules for manipulating those symbols. Those symbols represent quantities without fixed values, known as variables. Algebra forms the foundation for almost all higher mathematics and is essential in science, engineering, and everyday problem-solving.

💡 Key idea: In algebra, letters like x, y, and z stand in for unknown numbers. The goal is usually to find what value of the variable makes an equation true.

What is a Variable?

A variable is a symbol (usually a letter) used to represent an unknown or changeable value. For example, in the expression x + 5, the letter x is a variable — it can take on different values such as 2, 10, or −3.

Expressions vs. Equations

An expression is a combination of numbers, variables, and operations (like + or ×) that does not contain an equals sign. An equation states that two expressions are equal, using the = sign.

Example
Expression:    3x + 7
Equation:     3x + 7 = 16

Solving a Linear Equation

A linear equation has one variable raised to the first power. Solving it means finding the value of the variable that makes the equation true. Use inverse operations to isolate the variable — whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other.

Step-by-step example — Solve: 2x + 4 = 12
Step 1: Subtract 4 from both sides
           2x = 8

Step 2: Divide both sides by 2
           x = 4

The General Form

The standard form of a linear equation in one variable is:

ax + b = c

where a, b, and c are known numbers and x is the unknown. The solution is x = (c − b) / a, provided a ≠ 0.

Why Does Algebra Matter?

Algebra appears everywhere: calculating the best phone plan, figuring out how long a road trip will take, balancing a budget, or writing a computer program. Mastering algebra unlocks every field that relies on logical reasoning and quantitative thinking.
